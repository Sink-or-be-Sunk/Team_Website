Week 3:

Date: 9/10/2021
Total hours: 15

Description of design efforts:
In the beginning of this week, the analog multiplexers I ordered arrived and I was able to jump into testing. Initially, I tested the functionality of the device by placing only one voltage dividers to the Y0 pin and grounding the three select lines. This not only assured me of the functionality of the chip, but also demonstrated the accuracy of the chip (which was within 15% of the expected output). From here, I continued testing by migrating the voltage divider to the other output pins and manipulating the select line values. One of my concerns about the analog multiplexers was going to be the precision required for the select lines; upon further review and testing the select line, 70% of the Vcc value and above is identifies as “High”, and 30% of Vcc and lower identifies as “low”. This provides more flexibility than I had predicted and will become extremely beneficial when progressing to debugging stages, as we will be able to adjust the supply voltages with resistors to fit according. Once I had a solidified understanding of the singular analog multiplexer, I added a secondary multiplexer to imitate a second row on the player’s ship-placing board. Moreover, taking this extra step helped me visualize the layout of the final game board. Overall, it was critical to have a sound understanding of the analog multiplexers, their capabilities, and their constraints. As we progress into the next steps of circuitry and PCB design, our team will be able to better predict the functionality of the multiplexers, and thus the overall ship detection aspect of our game.

This week I 3D printed a prototype destroyer boat (from a free .stl file found online). Ultimately, this was to help our team visualize the sizing constraints for each of the pegs. After printing, we realized that we may need some type of flat panel on the bottom to evenly attach the voltage dividers. Moreover, one of the biggest challenges of this project will be finding a method of connecting the voltage dividers (inside of the boats) to the circuitry beneath the board, while still having room for the LEDs beneath to shine through. Throughout the week I brainstormed different methods of housing the voltage dividers in the bottom of the boat to electrically connect to the attack board. One idea I had was to open a push button and use the housing unit to store a soldered voltage divider. This was successful in that it just barely fit two resistors but had shortcomings in terms of supply power and ground and blocking the LED from shining through the boat. Next week, I plan to attempt to create a PCB (From the Advanced Manufacturing Team in the ECE Shop) with just a simple voltage divider and a hole in the middle for the LED beneath to shine through. If this is successful, there will be a standardized size for each of the pegs and a more organized method of placing the boats into the attack board.

In order to complete Homework 4 – Component Analysis, I was responsible for identifying the proper IC to use for charging or recharging the battery. However, after discussing with members from the 477 Staff, it is equally, if not more important, to first identify the type of battery required. Thus, this required some very high-level approximations of the power required by different components. I looked primarily at the microcontroller and the LED matrix since these will have considerable current draws. In doing so, I realized the LEDs will require much more power than our team had realized and may lead us to needing an alternate method of powering the gameboards. Overall, this was crucial for our team to identify so we may address the arising constraints. Next week, our team will work together to determine which powering method will be the most convenient and effective for the user.

Next week there will be a continued emphasis on the user and gameboard interaction; I will investigate more methods of creating a safe, but electrically possible way for the boats to contain the voltage dividers. I will continue to use this time to familiarize myself with KiCad in preparation for creating the PCB layout.

Week 5:

Date: 9/24/2021
Total hours: 13

Description of design efforts:
Throughout Week 5, our team was demonstrated a specific focus on deciding our ship detection method. We had prototyped a couple different versions in the past (such as the voltage dividers and the individually PCB methods), but at this point we recognized the importance of nailing down a concept and refining it with the remaining time. With that being said, I prototyped a version of using DC plugs (barrel and barrel jacks) for the boat pegs and detection. The barrels would be glue into the boat and the jacks would be placed beneath the acrylic sheet. This was a great idea in concept: very clean looking, easy to assemble, and plugging in would be easy. Additionally, by wiring this similarly to a switch (connecting on the ground pin to ground and the reference – detect – pin to a pull up resistor) it was very evident when the barrel (boat) was detected. I wired up a couple different jacks and monitors the results with varying current and voltages using a DMM and Oscilloscope.

However, during our lab time we decided to go with a different method of using header pins and female headers, very similar to my initial prototyping but without the resistors. Our team came to this conclusion primarily because although the barrels where very easy to click into the jacks, they required significant force to remove from them. Ultimately, this would put significant strain on the PCB and board itself. This step in the prototyping process was critical because it helped narrow down our options and decide the best method going forward. Now, we have a concrete methodology that we will use, one that we decided on based on the previous prototyping attempts.

Additionally, I continued ordering more components such as LEDs, motor, housing unit, and more. I also spent time in the ECE Shop looking for parts already available and determining which parts can be adapted to our project to reduce the cost. One of the biggest finds was the ESP32 microcontrollers, which is very beneficial but also seeing it in person made me realize that we will require a solder stencil – something that if we waited until after we ordered the chip to realize could have seriously hindered our prototyping time.

When the housing unit arrived, I immediately began measuring and creating various layouts. I used pieces of paper and components we are incorporating to get a better understanding of the variety of layout options and distributions. This was also incredibly important for the Mechanical Overview, which I was responsible for this week. After playing with the dimensions of the housing unit a little more and understanding the space availability, our team has been more successful in compiling a realistic mental image of the final product.

The majority of my time devoted this week was focused on completing the Mechanical Overview. The most challenging part was completing the CAD design and the PCB Layout. To complete the PCB layout, I first used Powerpoint to get proper dimensions for each device. Once I had the exact sizes, I was able to use some of the drawing features to play with different Row PCB sizes to estimate the amount of space our team will need for connectors at the end of each row, as well as the daisy-chain type connections. This alone took me a few hours to ensure everything was as accurate and precise as possible, as we are dealing with relatively small dimensions and even just one inch can make the difference of one of the PCBs fitting our not.

Finally, the CAD designing was very challenging for me – particularly because I had never used a CAD software before. I tried three different CAD softwares to get the specific features I was looking for, but eventually settled on one and did the best I could to represent my imagined final product. During this time, it was mainly trial and error; I spent a lot of time researching how to do simple things such as “move a design” and differentiate terms such as “body” from “sketch”. While this process felt extremely tedious, it helped me envision the final product, and further portray that image to others. By spending the time completing this portion of the Mechanical Overview, I gained confidence in our spacing and housing unit’s dimensions, and most importantly identified which areas within the unit will be the most constrained, and potentially cause problems down the road if not precisely measured.

Overall, this week helped me become more comfortable with the physical layouts of the design, including the PCBs.
