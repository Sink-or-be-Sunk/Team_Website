For the main PCB, I initially helped by sorting through the rat’s nest and ultimately sorting the circuit subsystems into separate chunks. From here, it was easier for Mitch and me to take a closer look at the footprint pinouts and reassign pins on the ESP32 and relevant components. Additionally, I worked through some of our primary heat and footprint concerns. For instance, the MOSFET and LDO can create a significant amount of heat if not carefully considered. I did some basic power calculations provided on the LDO datasheet to determine the maximum power dissipated. After identifying the thermal concern from the power dissipated, I reached out to Joe for recommendations. Ultimately, the best course of action for this situation will be to include a large power plane surrounding the Vout pin on the LDO, covering it with vias, and placing another power plane on the other side. Another potential solution would be to use the 5V supply (VBus) for the LEDs, which would significantly reduce the load on the LDO while still providing sufficient voltage for the LEDs. As for the MOSFET, our team will go with a specific P-Channel with a much lower internal resistance, which will create a lower voltage drop and lower the risk of getting too hot. From a footprint point of view, this means we can use an upright MOSFET instead of a flat orientation.

For the Row PCB, I finalized the proper sizing of the board and spacing between the pegs and LEDs. In order to do so, I re-measured the box dimensions and provided some additional space allowance to each of the rows. To find proper spacing, I drew everything to scale and made exact measurements. Then, to double check, I utilized some additional lines in KiCad and spaced them an inch apart (to resemble grid lines) and double checked the measurements between the center of the pegs and each line. On top of this, since I had the exact measurements between the peg holes, I was able to use this to modify a previous CAD version of the boat to include the holes with exact placement. For this, I utilized the hand-drawn measurements I already had but took a couple millimeters from each side to ensure the boat would not overcrowd any grid squares. I included a small rectangular platform on the bottom of the ship in order to completely cover the LED in the farthest squares. This was a potential concern since the boat from the STL file we are using is very narrow and could leave some exposed LED. Additionally, the boat has been printed in a specific way to allow our team to place the small pegs inside the boat then glue the top on separately; this reduces risk of damaging our boats when drilling through them to place the pegs. Additionally, the sizing of the rectangular platform was highly successful as seen by how flush the pegs are with the bottom of the boat.

This week I learned a significant amount about KiCad and PCB Design and identified shortcuts in OnShape (CAD software) to facilitate boat modification. Looking ahead to the design review, my focus will be critiquing, finalizing, and debugging our PCB layout as well as completing the design review presentation. To help us isolate some of the USB to Serial problems, I started populating a new Main PCB (we only need 2 boards, I ordered 5) and directly fly-wired connections, from the TX and RX pins from where the USB to Serial IC should have been, to a breadboard prototype using the FTDI, USB to UART development board.

This seemingly worked temporarily, however at this point we were still having unpredictable outputs; sometimes everything appeared to be working nominally, and the next, everything has crashed. With that being said, Mitch did a lot more research into narrowing down the problem, and determined that we need to reassign/repurpose some of the connections to the ESP32. We discussed different ways to fly-wire the connections, which pins will need to include pull-down resistors (all of the input-only pins), and so on. On top of this, he helped me begin setting up the required tools for flashing the ESP32 so I can continue with this debugging process next week. Overall, we established a gameplan as to how to plan to fix some of the connection issues, and now we will just need more testing.

With this week being Thanksgiving Break, my responsibilities throughout the week were more spread across different aspects of our project. One of the biggest things I worked on was installing the required tools and software for the ESP32 for programming and flashing. Mitch helped me get everything working properly, however it took multiple attempts and hours to get everything running; even so, the current setup I have allows me to flash the software, but I run into many errors trying to build. To get around the building issue, Mitch was able to build the code on his end (to reflect pin adjustments) then send me the zipped file to flash and run the serial monitor. The pictures below exhibit proper flashing then proper monitoring.

The main reason for getting set up with the software side is so I could work on making the pin adjustments and testing. Moreover, Mitch discovered issues with some of the pins we had selected (some were input only, some were directly connected to memory) and we discussed possible solutions. In the process of making the changes, I soldered on a couple more components and pull down resistors and utilized the exposes JTAG pins and the couple extra debug pins we included to properly wire the keypad and LCD display. At first, the ESP32 would not flash, and we later determined it to be directly correlated to the motor subsystem. To fix this, I completely desoldered the mosfet and pull down resistors (since they were using the ground from the mosfet), since the motor line going into the mosfet comes from one of the pins not recommended for use on the ESP32 datasheet. Then, we were able to flash and test the setup.

Currently the keypad is responding to only a couple (incorrect) buttons being pressed and the LCD display is not turning on at tall. Today I spent substantial time investigating the keypad for possible sources of error with some help from Garrett. Eventually, it became apparent that the rows and columns were switched and needed to be changed in both the code and in the connections. This was because in the first setup, the rows were identifying button pushes instead of the columns, to provide input to the ESP32. To fix this, I adjusted the GPIO values on Garrett’s computer, then built and sent myself the zipped file to flash on my own computer. This still had only a couple of buttons being identified, however Garrett and I both believe it to be a result of the additional pull down resistors on a couple of the rows. So, I desoldered the pull down resistors, but did not have enough time to test it without them – this will be first on the list of things to do for next week.

Finally, early this week our clear gameboard covers for the Grid PCBs were cut and I was able to pick them up. They were not cut with the specific acrylic we had purchased which means the thickness is slightly off and it is much more clear than expected, however it still looks very clean. Next week I will be returning to the machine shop to ask for another cut (without the socket holes) for the top boards.

Overall, this week was still pretty productive, regardless of the break. Moving forward, the pressure to get the ESP32 programmed correctly is significant, however I believe our team will rise to the occasion.
